Chapter 9: Belonging

Eight days passed before he noticed they had passed.

Catch-side-pass-1 came together the way a problem comes together when you finally stop arguing with it. By Sol 53 the launch trajectory phase had cleared its own tolerance band, and Vasquez, with no ceremony, had taken his timing tweak directly into the cradle parameters and rewritten her acknowledgment-handshake without sending him a note. By Sol 55 the machine tech, whose name was Oren and who had a sardonic mouth and a slow patience with all things mechanical, had begun saving him a seat at the long galley table — not by gesture, just by the way the empty chair always happened to be the one nearest the bench. By Sol 56 Peter had stopped looking, in the morning, at the corner of his left display where the soft yellow warning flag used to live. There was no flag there. There had not been a flag there since Sol 50. He had stopped expecting to find one.

He could not, eight days in, remember exactly when the work had become his. Some morning between Sol 52 and Sol 55 he had opened the spec without bracing for it. Some afternoon he had argued with Vasquez about reference-frame hierarchy and won the argument and not noticed he had won. Some evening Kessler had stopped at his workstation on the way to somewhere else, looked at the catch-side trajectory cluster on the left display, and said, in the voice of a man passing a younger colleague in a hallway, *That cluster is doing everything I wanted it to do.* Peter had nodded, kept working, and noticed only later that night, walking back to his cabin, that he was happy.

On Sol 58, Kessler caught him in the corridor at 1830 and said, "Galley. You're behind on a dinner I owe you. Come."

The galley was warmer than it had been on the four mornings of Peter's first week. Six of the nine were there: Vasquez at one end, Oren and one of the lab assistants at the bench under the recyclers, an older woman from the materials team Peter had only ever nodded to, a quieter man Peter assumed was the supply pilot's relief, and Kessler at the head of the table, a glass of something amber and not very strong in front of him. The food was not better than the food on Sol 44 — the food on the base was never going to be better than the food on the base — but the company had reorganized the room.

Oren said, halfway through the meal and apropos of nothing, "Kamau. Tell me you do not, in the actual code, name your variables after stars."

"I do not name my variables after stars."

"Liar." Oren chewed. "You named the catch-side branch after a hand of cards."

"Catch-side-pass-1 is a software branch name."

"Pass-1 is what a poker player says before he raises in pass-2."

"Pass-1 is what an engineer says before he opens pass-2 and finds out the first one wasn't right."

The lab assistant snorted into her drink. Vasquez, at the far end of the table, said, "He's been arguing with the integrator about it for eight days. I've stopped reading his commit messages." Her tone was flat, but Peter saw, for the first time since he had met her, the corner of her mouth do something that, in another woman, would have been a smile. He did not point it out. He took a second helping of the unimpressive stew, and found, to his small interior surprise, that he was hungry.

When the table had thinned, Kessler set his glass down and tipped his head, a small economic gesture toward the corridor. "Walk me back. I want to show you something."

What he wanted to show Peter, when they were in his office with the door closed and the working terminal dark for the night, was a photograph.

He took it down off the wall without ceremony — Peter had seen it before, Sol 44, the one of the reef in shallow water lit from beneath — and he set it flat on the bare wood of the desk, beside the coral fragment on its glass stand. He did not turn on the desk lamp. The corridor light through the open transom was enough.

"Your grandfather," Kessler said. "On your resume. You said his name was Kamau Mwangi."

"Yes."

"Coastal Kenya. Mombasa or thereabouts."

"Mombasa."

"He would have been alive when this reef was alive."

Peter did not answer that. Kessler did not seem to require him to.

"I went to Earth six times," Kessler said, in the same easy register he had used at the table. "First in 2510, last in 2522. I lived there, on and off, five years cumulatively. I am, technically, one of the last three living men to have done significant fieldwork on the planet. The other two are dead now." He looked at the photograph without touching it. "I was a marine biologist before I was anything else. My grandmother was a marine biologist. Her name was Miriam. She told me, when I was small, about coral reefs and whale migrations and the sea at night where the water was alive — bioluminescence she had seen as a child in a village three meters underwater by the time I was twelve. She died when I was twelve. She was the most intelligent person I have known. The fragment on the desk is hers. Her village. She kept it on her writing table for fifty-one years, and after she died my mother kept it for me, and I have kept it on a desk wherever I have had a desk."

He paused. Peter, in the chair across the desk, felt the warmth come up under his ribs and stay there, the way it had stayed at Sol 50, except that this time it felt like grief, not relief, and he could not have said which of them it was grief for.

"On the last expedition," Kessler said, "we documented the dying of the Andaman Fragment. The reef. The last one. We watched the bleach line move three meters in seven days. I have a notebook from those three weeks. It is field notes, raw, no metaphor. I have not read it since 2523. When I came back to Mars, I wrote a paper that several journals declined to publish on the grounds that it was not science. I think they meant it was not bearable. They were not entirely wrong."

"What happened to your work?"

"Mars Government reclassified Earth as a resource extraction zone in 2525. My department was defunded the next quarter. I lost my chair at Olympus. My wife — who had stayed with me through the expeditions, which is more than I had any right to ask — left in 2527 because there was no longer a place to live and she was not, fairly, an evolutionary biologist. I rented a basement lab in Tharsis Industrial. I worked there for ten years on borrowed equipment, on what I could get foundation money for, which was not much, and on what I could not get foundation money for, which was the part of the work that mattered. In 2535 a private donor made me an offer that did not require me to defend a budget to a committee that had already decided what kind of planet was useful. The donor is a private estate. The estate is the controlling interest in this base. The base is the lab the basement lab grew up to be."

He smiled — small, dry, entirely without bitterness, which was the most disturbing thing about it.

"That is the long answer. The short answer is that I was a paleontologist of a sort, which is to say a man who specialized in counting the things humanity broke before they were finished evolving, and at some point, after enough years of counting, you either lie down in the count or you decide to do something the count cannot reach."

He picked up the coral fragment off its stand. It was the first time Peter had seen him touch it.

"I spent my life," Kessler said, "watching humanity destroy the most beautiful thing in the universe. This project is my chance to build something instead."

He set the fragment back on its stand. The room was quiet enough to hear the working terminal's cooling fan asleep.

Peter looked at the photograph on the desk. He did not look at Kessler.

He understood, in the small undeclared way one understands these things, that Kessler had told him this not because the project required it but because Kessler had been waiting, for longer than anyone should wait, for someone in the building who could be told it without being lost. He understood, in the same undeclared way, that the man across the desk was a possible future of the man in this chair — twenty years on, the same brilliance, the same isolation, the same set of hands on a desk in a borrowed room — and that the difference between them, the only difference, was that one of them had decided to do something and the other had spent six months in a Mars apartment closing job applications.

He did not say any of that. He said, "Thank you for telling me."

"Thank you for being someone I could tell." Kessler stood. "Sleep, Peter. Tomorrow's the integration window with Vasquez's cradle. I'll need you sharp."

"I'll be sharp."

"And Peter — " Kessler paused at the office door, hand on the frame, the expedition photographs ranked behind him in the corridor light. He did not look back. "We'll talk about the third thing soon."

Peter walked back to the Nav Lab through corridors that were sleeping. The residential level had the soft hum of recyclers cycling night air. The gray-flour bay was closed. He passed the door without slowing.

At his workstation, he did not sit immediately. He stood with his hand on the back of the chair and let the day land. Then he sat, opened catch-side-pass-1, and did what he had been doing for eight nights — one quiet pre-bed look at the launch trajectory cluster, a routine stellar position consistency check, the kind of check a navigation programmer runs on his own work because he is the kind of navigation programmer who runs checks on his own work.

The check returned the residuals he expected. The cluster held under tolerance.

He sat with the result for a long second, the way he had sat in Kessler's office twenty minutes before.

Then, on a habit of attention he could not have explained at the time, he asked the integrator a slightly different question — not whether the cluster held, but how tightly the reference epochs themselves were distributed across the stellar position catalogue. The cover story called for a generic envelope, several million years deep, tolerant of orbital decoherence past Jupiter's chaos horizon, deliberately uncorrelated with the present so as not to paradox itself.

The reference epochs were not distributed across that envelope.

They were clustered. Tightly. Around a single point in deep time, with a residual scatter narrower than the cover story's design tolerance permitted. Not a smear. A line.

He looked at it for a count of seconds that he later could not remember the length of.

Then, without opening another tab, without saying anything to himself in words, he reached up and pulled down the stellar-cartography comparison tool from the menu — the tool he had not used since Helix, the tool he had not run on this base because there had been no reason to — and queried the cluster's barycentric center against the historical epoch catalogues he had loaded onto his datapad eight years ago and never deleted.

The catalogues were dense. The query was specific.

A small progress bar opened in the corner of his left display, where the warning flag used to live, and began, quietly, to load.
