Chapter 11: The Decision

Sol 70 found him at the long galley table at 0640, two mornings on the other side of the night his hands had stopped, and he ate the same dehydrated paste he had eaten every morning since the year had become a number he was no longer counting from.

Sol 68 day had passed. He had been at the integration window. He had been in the corridor. He had answered three messages and one of them had been a one-line congratulations from Vasquez on a clean residual run. Sol 69 had been a longer day inside a shorter one. He had performed the catch-side-pass-1 final rebalance Kessler had asked him to deliver before the end of the week. The integrator had not complained. He had walked back through the gray-flour bay corridor in the dimmed cycle and the door had been closed and he had not slowed.

The screen corner where the warning flag and then the progress bar had lived was still blank when he opened his terminal at 0700. He looked at the blank for the count it always took him now and turned to the second-half spec without naming what he was looking for in the empty space.

The unwritten conditional had been the shape of a thing in the next room for forty-eight hours and had not gotten smaller.

Oren came in at 0715 with two cups of the grey coffee and put one down beside Peter's plate.

"You ate the paste," Oren said. "Voluntarily. I saw you do it."

"It was there."

"That is a thing you can say about most ground glass." Oren sat down. "The cradle made a noise last night."

"Bad noise?"

"Good noise. The kind it makes when it doesn't make any of the other noises." Oren sipped his coffee. "Vasquez says we are within tolerance. She used the word *tolerance* twice in one sentence. It was nearly a poem."

Peter did not laugh. Something in his chest did the thing a laugh would have done and stopped at his throat. He looked at the blue stain on the side of Oren's coffee cup, the one Oren had put there four mornings ago by setting it down on the unwiped corner of a table Peter had not yet learned to wipe, and he understood, for one count, that he had decided he was going to remember the cup.

He did not finish the thought. He did not need to.

"Drink your coffee, Kamau," Oren said. "Today's a day."

"It is?"

"The Professor's been writing the date on his hand for a week. Today's the day."

Kessler called them all to the briefing room at 1000.

It was the first time the whole base had been in one space since the day Peter had arrived. Six chairs had been set out and the rest of them stood. Vasquez at the back with her datapad against her chest. The Handler with his hands behind his back, in the way of a man who expected to be looked at and intended not to be remembered. The two students near the door, very young, very awake. Oren on the bench against the wall. Diaz the supply pilot, returned this week from a Mars run, leaning in the doorway. Peter against the far wall, where he could see Kessler's hands.

Kessler stood at the front of the room without notes. The coral fragment was on the table beside him on its glass stand. He had brought it.

"Ten days," he said.

He let them have it for a count of seconds.

"On Sol 80, at 0413 base time, the Cauchy window opens for seven milliseconds. The cradle is integrated. The accelerator is calibrated. The seed is loaded. Vasquez tells me her output frame is referenced against the trajectory frame to better than two milliseconds across the bus. We are inside tolerance. We have been inside tolerance for nine days. On Sol 80 we will inject."

The Handler did not change. Vasquez nodded once, already calculating. Diaz let out a small quiet whistle. The students looked at each other in the way of people who had not believed the thing they had believed until someone announced it.

"I would like," Kessler said, "to say what I am supposed to say, which is *thank you*. I have been in a room with people who made things their entire lives. None of those rooms were this room. The work we are doing on this base is the only thing of weight I have done in twelve years. Some of you will know what I mean. Some of you will not yet. Either way." He paused. "Either way, I am grateful to all of you, and I would like the next ten days to be ten days during which we do the work the way it has been done, and not ten days during which I make speeches about it." He almost smiled. "So. Ten days. Vasquez has the schedule. Get her what she needs."

That was the announcement. Peter held his face. Inside, the frame inverted once — *the future we are building* — and he heard, beneath the warmth in the room, the long silence of a planet on which his species had never spoken. He let the inversion pass without sitting on it. He had done that work two nights ago.

The room broke up the way rooms break up after that kind of speech — quietly, without congratulations exactly, but with a small surplus of attention each person carried to the next thing they did. Vasquez intercepted him in the corridor before he had taken three steps.

"Kamau."

"Yes."

"I want a full walkthrough of the nav stack before lockdown. Launch trajectory phase, cradle handshake, interceptor approach geometry, end to end. No surprises on Sol 80." She looked at her datapad. "I have you on Sol 75 at 1400. Five days. Get me a clean build."

"Sol 75 at 1400."

"Clean build, Kamau."

"I'll have a clean build."

She nodded once, already walking, already three steps into the next decision she was making. Peter watched her go and did not move for a count.

Five days.

He went to Kessler's office in the late afternoon because Kessler had asked him to come, and because not going would have been the wrong shape. The office was as it had been on Sol 58. The paper books on three walls. The Earth photographs on the fourth. The working terminal had a half-rendered trajectory simulation in the corner that Peter recognized as his own. The coral fragment was back on its glass stand on the bare wood of the desk.

"Sit, Peter. Coffee?"

"I've had two."

"You've always had two." Kessler smiled. He looked tired in a way Peter had not seen before — not lacking sleep, but the particular tiredness of a man who has spent a long time getting a thing to a particular condition and has now been told the thing is in that condition. He sat down across from Peter and did not start with work. "I wanted to tell you, before the rest of the week, that the decision to add the catch-side phase to the spec — to widen the architecture so that one navigation programmer would be holding both ends of the integration — that was something I argued for against the rest of the team. They wanted to silo. I said no, the work has to be coherent. I am glad it was you who took it." He paused. "I am sometimes afraid that I will not have been the right person to do this. It helps to have been in a room with someone who would have been."

Peter did not answer. He could not answer. He was looking at the coral fragment on its glass stand and the line of warm light across Kessler's hand where it rested near it, and he was understanding, the way one understands these things in the small undeclared way, that he had sat across from this man twice before and seen him both times — the man on Sol 58 who had told him the truth and the man on Sol 67 who had been preparing the third thing — and that in neither of those rooms had he seen what he was seeing now, which was a man in his last week of being unopposed.

Kessler's hand, by the fragment, was steady. The fingers did not touch the coral. They lay near it the way a man's hand lay near a child he was not going to wake. Peter had seen those hands two weeks ago, on Sol 58, pick the fragment up off its stand for the first time. He had not seen them set it back so carefully. The care was new. The care was for the next ten days.

The conditional was no longer a shape in the next room.

"Thank you, Arthur."

"Get her her clean build."

"I will."

He walked back to the Nav Lab through corridors that hummed with the day cycle. The gray-flour bay door was open and the man with the fingerless gloves was at his station. Peter looked into the bay for a count of seconds longer than he had on any prior day. The medium under the containment glass moved the way it always moved, slow and patient, breathing on a clock he had been hired to set.

He sat down at his workstation. He opened catch-side-pass-1 and did not touch it. He read through the launch trajectory phase the way Vasquez would read it, which was the way he had been reading it for ten weeks. He noted, without writing it down, the three places where a small adjustment to the integrator's reference-frame priority would, downstream, propagate — not in any way Vasquez's review would catch, because Vasquez's review would catch what the spec said the code should do, and the spec was what the code would, in those three places, still appear to do.

He did not write any of those places into a notepad. He had practice in not writing things down.

He worked through the rest of the afternoon on the rebalance. He took dinner with Oren and the supply pilot and answered the two questions Diaz asked him about Mars weather in the long quiet of someone who had been off-base for three weeks and wanted to know what he had missed. He laughed once. Not at a joke. At Oren's face when Oren saw the dessert was the same paste with sugar in it.

At 2210 he was alone in the Nav Lab. He did the close-of-day on his workstation. The screen corner was blank. He looked at it the way he had looked at it that morning, without naming what he was looking for, and turned out the lamp.

The corridor on the way to his bunk was on its dim cycle. The observation port at the far end of the residential level showed the same wedge of stars he had seen on Sol 68, advanced two days. Vega. The bright pair of Alpha Centauri. The long faint smear of the Milky Way at the angle his neck remembered. The Pleiades just out of frame to the left, where they had been then and where they would be for the next ten days.

He stood at the port for a count he later remembered as long and watched them.

He had had the knowledge for four days. He still had the access. He had the one skill that could stop the plan, and now he had the deadline that would make him use it. The certainty he had not had two nights ago had arrived without ceremony, in a room he had been sitting in for the wrong reasons, in the warm light across a man's hand near a piece of coral that had outlived everyone who had loved it.

The Pleiades were where they had been. They would be where they were on Sol 80, and on the day after Sol 80, and on every day after that. He had not yet earned the right to think about which of those futures he was choosing for them. He had five days, and a clean build, and the rest of his life inside one of two skies.

He did not have a plan yet. He had five days to write one.

He went to his bunk.
