Chapter 2: Missing Nanomachines

The warehouse loading dock smelled like hydraulic fluid and stale coffee, which suited Bob Harmon just fine most days. Today was not most days. Today he had a date with Mary Chen at the new Szechuan place in Dome 4, and Steve was taking his sweet goddamn time with the inventory check.

"Come on, Steve, it's all here." Bob glanced at his watch for the third time in two minutes. "We've been doing this every two weeks for three years. I've never missed anything. You're making me late."

Steve Kowalski didn't look up from his tablet. He stood at the sorting table, methodically scanning each item in the delivery crate against the manifest, his lips moving silently as he cross-referenced serial numbers. The warehouse hummed around them — automated forklifts gliding along floor tracks, ventilation fans cycling the dome's recycled air, the distant clang of a cargo container being sealed three bays over.

"I don't want to lose my job because you're feeling lovey-dovey," Steve said, tapping his stylus against the next item. "I have to check and log everything. Every tube, every seal, every—"

He stopped.

Bob's heart sank. "What? What is it?"

Steve held up a glass tube, about fifteen centimeters long and as thick as his thumb. A hairline fracture ran its length, barely visible unless you caught the light at the right angle. He turned it slowly, and the crack caught the fluorescent strip above them, glinting like a thread of spider silk.

"The glass tube is broken." Steve's voice had gone flat. "All the others are fine. Must have been damaged during packaging."

Bob leaned in. The tube looked empty — just clear glass, sealed at both ends. "What's in there? They all look empty to me."

Steve consulted the manifest on his tablet, scrolling through the itemized list. "Let's see... lot number 7741-C, row three, position fourteen. They're labeled as 'self-replicating asteroid mining machines.'"

"What are you talking about? I'm looking right at it. There's nothing in there."

"They're nanomachines, Bob. You can't see them." Steve's irritation gave way to something else — the particular enthusiasm of a man who read too many tech journals. "They're the latest thing. Way cheaper than the old method. Instead of loading a mining rig onto a cargo ship and flying it out to the belt, they just fire these tubes from a cannon. Like a bullet. The tube smashes on the asteroid's surface, and thousands of nanomachines crawl out. They find resources, replicate themselves, build bigger machines, conduct geological surveys, refine raw materials into ingots, and ship the ingots back to Moon Station 19. All automated. No human crew needed."

Bob whistled despite himself. "So as long as one of them survives the landing, it just... starts working?"

"Exactly. Takes forever with just one, so they shoot dozens of tubes to speed things up. But yeah — one is enough. Given enough time, a single nanomachine can bootstrap an entire mining operation from scratch."

"Can we scoop them back into the tube?"

Steve shook his head. "Doesn't matter. They're harmless without activation. Designed to function only in specific asteroid-surface environments and require a complex activation sequence. Without it, they're just dust. Fancy dust, but dust."

Relief washed over Bob's face. "Alright. Sounds really cool. But I'm not taking the fall for this — there's no damage on the outer case, nothing happened during the trip. This was a packaging issue."

"Yeah, relax. I'll log it as damaged during packaging. They can request replacements in the next batch." Steve was already typing the entry, his stylus moving in quick, practiced strokes. "No harm done."

"Thanks, man. I owe you one." Bob was already backing toward the bay door, pulling his jacket off the hook. "Beer next time. My treat."

"Sure thing." Steve waved him off without looking up.

The bay door sealed behind Bob, and the warehouse settled into its usual quiet. Steve finished the log entry, set down his tablet, and stood still for a moment, listening to the hum of the ventilation system.

Then he reached under the sorting table and pulled out a small handheld vacuum — not the warehouse's standard-issue model, but a compact, high-filtration unit he'd brought from home. He placed the cracked tube into a sealed evidence bag, switched on the vacuum, and carefully swept the interior of the delivery crate where the broken tube had sat. The process took less than two minutes. Whatever invisible particles had leaked from the fracture were now trapped in the vacuum's collection chamber.

He placed the vacuum in his backpack, slung the pack over his shoulder, and clocked out for the evening. The security camera at the bay exit recorded him leaving at 17:42, carrying the same backpack he always carried. Nothing unusual. Nothing worth flagging.

---

The man was already waiting when Steve arrived at the service corridor behind the water reclamation plant. It was the kind of place where the dome's infrastructure made enough noise to swallow a conversation — pumps cycling, pipes groaning, condensation dripping from overhead ducts.

Steve had met him only once before. A man named Joe — or at least, that's the name he'd given. Joe had found Steve three weeks ago, which was unsettling in itself. Steve had never figured out how Joe knew about him, or how he'd gotten his private comm address, or how he'd known about the one thing Steve wanted more than anything — the one thing his warehouse salary could never buy, not legally, not on Mars.

"It's all here," Steve said, holding out the vacuum's collection chamber. His voice was steady but his hands weren't. "I followed your instructions. Used the vacuum you gave me. Sucked up everything from the crate. The computer logs show the tube was damaged during packaging. No one can trace this back to me."

Joe took the chamber without inspection and handed it to the man standing in the shadows behind him.

Steve had not been told about a second person.

The man stepped forward just enough to accept the chamber. He was tall, lean, and utterly unremarkable in the way that people who do not wish to be remembered often are. He pulled out a portable microscope, placed a sample slide under the lens, and examined it for a long, silent moment. At the bottom of the chamber, a few thousand dormant nanomachines lay settled like fine sediment — lifeless, inert, waiting for an activation process that Steve could not have provided even if he'd known how.

The man nodded once. Satisfied.

"Now," Steve said, his voice dropping, "give me what you promised."

Joe reached behind a pipe housing and produced a small box with holes punched in the top. Steve took it with both hands, carefully, as if it contained something made of glass.

A hamster poked its head out.

It was small and brown, with round black eyes and ears so tiny they were almost translucent. It looked up at Steve and twitched its nose.

Steve's face transformed. The tension, the fear, the calculated risk of the past three weeks — all of it dissolved into something soft and almost childlike. He cupped the box against his chest.

"Why do you want that thing?" Joe asked, watching with undisguised bewilderment. "I heard they chew on cables and make a mess everywhere. You know they can lock you up for a hundred sols just for having one?"

"I've always wanted one," Steve said quietly. "Since I was a kid. Look at its tiny ears."

Joe stared at him for a moment, then shrugged. "Whatever. I'm going to burn all the DNA traces on my hands and in my apartment. You should do the same."

He turned and disappeared into the service corridor, his footsteps fading into the rhythmic thud of the reclamation pumps.

Steve stood alone with his hamster, running one finger gently over its head. It closed its eyes and leaned into his touch. He had no idea what the nanomachines were for, and he did not care. For the first time in years, something warm and alive depended on him, and that was enough.

---

Joe found the man at the rendezvous point — a disused freight elevator shaft at the edge of the dome's industrial sector. The man was examining the nanomachine sample under his microscope again, turning the slide with slow, precise movements.

"Why do you even want these things?" Joe asked, leaning against the elevator frame. "They're useless without activation. Fancy dust, like the warehouse guy said."

The man looked up. A cryptic smile played at the corners of his mouth — the kind of smile that suggested the question itself was amusing rather than the answer.

"That will be my problem, right?"

"Whatever." Joe pocketed the credit chip the man had left on the railing. The amount was generous — more than generous. The kind of money that made a man stop asking questions. "As long as I'm getting paid. Nice doing business with you."

He walked away, counting the credits on his wrist display. The number was good. Very good. But he couldn't quite shake the feeling — the one that settled in his stomach like bad food — that he'd just helped set something in motion that was larger, and darker, and far more dangerous than a box of invisible dust.

Behind him, the man sealed the nanomachine chamber in a containment case, placed it in a nondescript courier bag, and walked in the opposite direction. He did not look back. He did not need to. The first piece was secured.

The other pieces were already in progress.
