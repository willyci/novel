Chapter 2: Missing Nanomachines

Bob glanced at his watch, anxiety creeping into his voice. "Come on, Steve, it's all here. We've been doing this every two weeks for three years. I've never missed anything!" He ran a hand through his unkempt hair, thinking about his impending date. "You're making me late for my date with Mary!"

Steve, ever the stickler for protocol, mumbled as he meticulously checked each item. "I don't want to lose my job because you're feeling lovey-dovey! I have to check and log everything."

Suddenly, Steve's methodical process came to an abrupt halt. "Oh shit," he muttered, his face paling.

Bob's heart sank. "What is it? I didn't break anything, not even hit a bump on the road. Don't pin this on me."

Steve held up a glass tube, a hairline fracture running its length. "The glass tube is broken. All the others are fine. It must have been damaged during packaging."

Bob peered at the tube, confusion etched on his face. "What's in there? They all look empty to me."

Sighing, Steve consulted his inventory list. "Let's see... ah, here it is. They're 'self-replicating asteroid mining machines.'"

"What are you talking about? Those glass tubes look empty!" Bob exclaimed, his patience wearing thin.

"They're nanomachines, you can't see them," Steve explained, a hint of excitement creeping into his voice. "They're the latest tech. Super cheap to deploy to any asteroid's surface compared to the old way. Instead of sending bulky machines via spaceship, they just fire these from a cannon, like a bullet. Once it smashes on the asteroid surface, thousands of nanomachines emerge. They search and gather resources, replicate themselves, build bigger machines, conduct surveys, refine materials, and ship ingots back to Mars. All automated. It's going to replace all the space mining jobs soon."

Bob whistled, impressed despite his urgency to leave. "As long as one of them survives the landing, it can start working?"

Steve nodded. "Exactly. It takes forever with just one, so they shoot quite a few tubes to speed things up."

"Can we scoop them back into the tube?" Bob asked, hopeful for a quick solution.

"Nah, don't worry. They're harmless," Steve reassured him. "Designed to function only in specific environments and require a complex activation process. Without activation, they're just like dust."

Relief washed over Bob's face. "Alright, alright, sounds really cool. But I'm not taking the fall for this. There's no damage on the case, nothing happened on the trip."

"Yeah, relax," Steve said, already updating his log. "I'll just record it as damaged during packaging. They can ask for more in the next batch."

"Thanks, man. I owe you one," Bob said, already backing towards the door. "Let me buy you a beer next time."

"Sure thing," Steve replied, waving him off.

---

"It's all here," Steve said. "I followed your instructions, used the vacuum you gave me. Sucked up everything. No one can tell, all the computer logs are legit. No one can trace this back to me."




The man standing in the shadows nodded, then pulled out a portable microscope. He carefully examined the tube, noting the few thousand dormant nanomachines settled at the bottom, lifeless and waiting for activation.

The man goes by Joe, Steve only meets him the second time, has no idea how he found him, and knows everything about him, contacts him, and tells him that he can get what he always wanted, not with his salary on the black market plus 100 sols in jail. 

"Now," Steve said, his voice tinged with anticipation, "give me what you promised."
Without a word, the man handed over a small box with holes on the top. Bob opened it gingerly, and a hamster poked its head out, its tiny eyes wide with fear.

Joe watching from the sidelines, couldn't help but interject. "Why do you want that thing? I heard they chew on cables and make a mess everywhere. They can lock you up for 100 sols for it."
Steve's face softened as he gazed at the creature. "I've always wanted one since I was a kid. Look at its tiny ears!"

Joe shrugged, already turning to leave. "Whatever. I'm going to burn all the DNA traces on my hands and in my apartment. you should do the same!"

Joe disappeared into the Martian night, and Steve cradled his new pet, oblivious to the potential consequences of their transaction. 

---

Joe called out, "Why do you even want these nanomachines? They're useless without activation."

The man paused, a cryptic smile playing on his lips. "That will be my problem, right?"

"Whatever," Joe muttered, pocketing his payment. "As long as I'm getting paid. Nice doing business with you!"

Meanwhile, Joe counted his credits, pushing away the nagging feeling that they might have just set something dangerous in motion.
