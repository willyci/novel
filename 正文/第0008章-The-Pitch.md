Chapter 8: The Pitch

Sol 45 came and went without him asking.

He had risen at 0530, dressed, gone to the galley, and walked the corridor to the Nav Lab the way he had walked it the four mornings before, and at no point in the eighty-three minutes between waking and sitting down at the workstation had he gone left at the central junction toward Kessler's office. He had told himself, as he opened the spec, that he would go after the morning's commit. After the morning's commit he had told himself he would go after he closed the warning flag. He had closed the warning flag eleven times by 1100 and walked back to the residential level for lunch by way of the corridor that did not pass Kessler's door.

By Sol 47 the notepad had migrated faceup onto the desk beside the keyboard, then facedown again, then back into the drawer. By Sol 48 he was rehearsing the conversation in the shower. The rehearsal was thorough. He had the precise wording of his three questions, the order in which he would deliver them, and three different ways to ask the second one without sounding like he was accusing anyone of anything. He had a rebuttal prepared for an answer Kessler had not yet given. By Sol 49 the rehearsal had become a small daily ritual, like the closing of the warning flag, and by Sol 50 he understood, without admitting it in those words, that he had begun rehearsing in order not to ask.

He committed one more refactor at 1340 on Sol 50. He stood up. He took the notepad out of the drawer, opened it to the page with the three lines, did not turn it over, and walked to Kessler's office before he could find another reason to commit.

Samir was not in the corridor. The gray-flour containment bay, when he passed it, was closed. He had no idea whether that meant the work had moved or paused or whether it had simply been closed for an hour. The corridor was warmer than he remembered.

Kessler's door was open. Kessler was at the desk, reading something on the working terminal, and looked up at the soft knock with a small, unsurprised smile.

"Peter. Come in. Coffee?"

"No, thank you."

"How did the integrator behave overnight?"

"It held. The cluster you've been arguing with stayed under tolerance through the third epoch."

"I noticed." Kessler gestured at the chair across the desk, then at the working terminal, where Peter's perturbation model was running quietly in the corner the way it had been running four mornings before. "Sit. What can I do for you?"

Peter set the notepad on the desk between them, faceup. He did not sit yet. He had decided, in the corridor, that he would deliver the questions standing, and he kept to it.

"Three things, Arthur." His voice came out flatter than he meant. He let it. "The nav code is required to maintain stellar drift correction across reference frames spanning, in places, millions of years. There is no asteroid mining trajectory in the catalogue of human spaceflight that has ever needed that. I would like to understand why this one does. That's the first question. The second is what kind of mining trajectory does need that. The third is why I had to come down here to ask."

Kessler read the notepad without picking it up. He read it twice. The pleased silence that followed was small and contained, the silence of a man who has been waiting through a particular weather and feels the wind change.

"Sit down, Peter. I've been hoping you'd ask."

Peter sat.

Kessler closed the working terminal. Behind him, the framed Earth photographs caught the office light at the angle they had caught it four mornings before. The coral fragment on its glass stand was where it had been. Kessler folded his hands on the desk, the way another kind of executive would unfold them to begin a sales pitch, and began in the opposite register entirely — slowly, conversationally, the way one tells a colleague something one has been waiting a long time to be allowed to say.

"You know what the trillion-credit problem in asteroid mining has always been, Peter."

"Wait time."

"Wait time. From the moment a Belt prospector flags an ingot-grade body to the moment refined metal is in an Earth-orbital warehouse: nine to fourteen years for the cheap routes, four to six for the expensive ones, never less than two even with full Mars-Belt logistics under near-perfect window alignment. Investors price discount that wait time at twelve to seventeen percent annually, compounded. Nobody has ever broken it. Several people have tried. Nobody has even meaningfully *narrowed* it. It is the structural law of solar-system extraction. You cannot move mass faster than physics will let you move it, and physics, on those scales, is not inclined to negotiate."

"I'm familiar."

"I'm sure you are. So when I tell you we are going to deliver refined ingots to Earth orbit on demand — zero wait time, with the fulfillment latency of a same-day courier — you have, professionally, exactly two reactions. One: I am lying. Two: I have done something to physics."

Peter did not answer that. Kessler did not seem to expect him to.

"The second is closer."

The room was quiet enough that Peter could hear the working terminal's cooling fan.

"The mechanism is a Kerr black hole," Kessler said. "Ours is small. It is held in a Casimir-stabilized exotic-matter cradle — what you have been hearing called Sun 2.0 around the base, which is a bit of marketing I regret and have not yet won the argument over. The cradle stabilizes the Cauchy horizon long enough to inject a single payload into a closed timelike curve through the ergosphere — frame-dragging carries it in, geodesic injection lines it up with the Lense-Thirring exit profile on the far side. The window is seven milliseconds before mass inflation reasserts and we have to release the cradle. The payload is one nanomachine, a tenth of a gram. It lands in the deep past." He paused. "Several million years deep, on the launch we use. Not in geological terms. In trajectory-design terms. Far enough that the orbital perturbation environment is statistically uncorrelated with the present, so we do not paradox ourselves."

Peter had stopped breathing for one of those millions-of-years intervals and started again before Kessler noticed.

"How do you anchor the reference frame across the CTC exit?"

"You don't. You anchor at the entrance, you let the integrator carry the drift as residual, and you converge on the exit anchor when the nanomachine takes its first stellar fix on the other side. Which is where your code becomes interesting. The drift envelope you've been working in is the envelope of *uncertainty* on the exit side. Your job is to give the nanomachine the cleanest stellar fix it can take in the first six hours of its life on the other end." He tipped one hand. "That's the launch trajectory phase."

"And the catch?"

"That's the second half of your code. The nanomachine is a von Neumann probe. It replicates from local material — there is no shortage of suitable matter several million years deep in this solar system, and a single nanomachine becomes a swarm in the geological blink. The swarm mines. The swarm refines. The swarm casts ingots, and the swarm launches those ingots on long ballistic trajectories — million-year trajectories, give or take — that arrive, on schedule, in present-day near-Kuiper space. We catch them with orbital interceptors. The interceptor approach geometry is in the second half of your spec. You have not opened that section yet."

"I have not."

"You will. The integration tolerance is the same. The gravitational perturbation modeling is the same. The reference frames are reversed. You are calculating how a payload arrives on a present-day catch envelope after a million-year ballistic flight whose departure point was computed by the same code that put the seed nanomachine where it needed to be. The same code, Peter. End to end. The trillion-credit logistics chain inside one navigation stack."

"Zero wait time," Peter said.

"Zero wait time. The shipment arrived a million years ago. The shipment arrives this morning. Both are true."

Peter sat with that for a moment.

He could feel the spec rearranging itself in his head. The reference frames spanning millions of years were not a misprint or a redundancy or a paranoiac's tolerance buffer. They were the launch trajectory side. The autonomous operation windows beyond commercial scope were not beyond commercial scope; they were the von Neumann window between seeding and launch. The integration tolerance Vasquez had called "higher than you'll need" was the headroom for a seven-millisecond exotic-matter cradle holding a Cauchy horizon open. The bizarre architecture was not bizarre. It was a logistics chain whose first leg ran backward through deep time.

"The interceptors," he said. "Where are they staged?"

"Distributed. Several inner-Kuiper, several near-Earth. A handful on solar-orbit station-keeping at L4 and L5. We are not the only operators with infrastructure in those windows, and we like the company — it makes us less interesting." Kessler watched him without watching him. "The approach geometry is what it is because the inbound trajectories are, individually, very old. They have been in flight long enough that the present-day catch corridor has to be calculated against the ingot's entire memory of solar-system perturbation. Jupiter's chaos has a horizon past which you can't backtrack with confidence — past a certain point the orbital state space simply decoheres — and the launch trajectories are deliberately threaded so the catch leg is short enough that the chaos hasn't smeared them beyond your tolerance. That's a piece of trajectory design I am, frankly, very pleased with. We were the first to thread it."

There was a small wrongness in Kessler's cadence on Jupiter's chaos. The same wrongness Peter had heard, in a smaller key, on Sol 44. Peter held the silence for a breath, the way he had been holding it for six days, and then he let the breath go.

"Why did I have to come down here to ask?"

Kessler smiled. It was a small, entirely real smile.

"Because three other people on the team know the architecture end to end. Vasquez knows the cradle and the ergosphere geometry. The team in the bay you keep slowing down at knows the von Neumann growth curves. Until you understand what the nav code is for, you are doing a piece of work that has no shape. I do not believe in giving people shapeless work. I also do not believe in delivering this conversation before the person across from me has earned it." He gestured at the notepad. "You earned it on Sol 44. You spent six days deciding whether you wanted it. That is not an accusation. That is a competence. I have hired people who would have asked on Sol 41. They are not here anymore."

The warmth came up under Peter's ribs and this time he did not try to intercept it. It rose, sat in his chest, and stayed.

"How long until the first commercial shipment?"

"Eighteen months. We are integrating now."

"What do you need from me by the end of the quarter?"

"The launch trajectory phase, fully tested across the epoch envelope, with Vasquez's cradle parameters integrated. Then the interceptor approach geometry. Then we will talk about the third thing." He stood up and offered his hand. "Welcome to the project, Peter. The actual project."

Peter shook his hand. The room had not changed. The coral fragment had not moved. The Earth photographs were where they had been on Sol 44.

"Dinner this week," Kessler said. "The galley does us no favors."

"I'd like that."

He walked back to the Nav Lab through corridors that were no longer unfamiliar. He passed the gray-flour bay; the door was open again, and the man with the fingerless gloves was at his station, and Peter understood, now, what the small, patient breathing of the medium meant — a swarm under construction, on its way to a million-year working life on the far side of a seven-millisecond window. He looked at it for a second longer than he had on Sol 44, and what he felt was not unease. It was admiration.

At his workstation, he did not sit immediately. He stood for a moment with his hand on the back of the chair. Then he sat, opened the spec to the second half — the half he had not yet read — and began to read it without the warning flag in the corner of his attention.

The reference epoch envelope was the launch trajectory phase. The autonomous operation window was the von Neumann growth phase. The integration tolerance was Vasquez's cradle headroom. The second half of the spec was the interceptor approach geometry, exactly as Kessler had said. The architecture, end to end, was beautiful, and it was his.

He started a new branch. He named it *catch-side-pass-1*. He began refactoring under the new model, and the model held — not haltingly, the way an unfamiliar architecture holds, but cleanly, the way a model holds when its author has finally been told what the work is for.

Somewhere in the next twenty minutes the soft yellow warning flag at the corner of his left display went out. It did not flicker. It simply cleared. Peter, his hands moving fast over the keyboard, did not notice it go.
