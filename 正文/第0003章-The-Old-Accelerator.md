Chapter 3: The Old Accelerator

The Prometheus Particle Research Facility had seen better days. Tucked behind the sprawling Helios Power Station on the outskirts of Olympus City, the building wore its age like a badge of reluctant service — cracked thermal tiles along the exterior, a loading dock that groaned when the hydraulic doors opened, and a reception area where the holographic directory had been stuck on the wrong floor for at least two years.

Marcus Webb didn't mind. He'd managed this facility for eleven years, outlasting three corporate owners and two government audits. The accelerator itself — a Kepler-7 model, decommissioned from active research but still fully operational — was his pride. Not the flashiest machine on Mars, but she was reliable.

Today — Sol 8 of the new quarter, a crisp Martian morning — he had an appointment that made him vaguely uneasy, though he couldn't have said why. Dr. Hadron's office had contacted him two weeks ago. The university wanted to acquire a mid-range accelerator for an upcoming project. Exotic particle research, they said. Hadron himself was too busy to come, so he was sending an associate. A Dr. Freeman. Marcus had never heard the name, which was unusual — he knew most of the active particle physicists on Mars, at least by reputation.

He checked his reflection in the darkened monitor, straightened his collar, and walked to the entrance.

The man who stepped through the airlock was not what Marcus expected. Tall, lean, with the kind of posture that suggested years of academic conferences rather than lab work. His jacket was immaculate — not the rumpled tweed of a working physicist, but the crisp cut of someone who attended funding committees. He carried a thin briefcase and wore an expression of polite, focused interest.

"Dr. Freeman, I presume?" Marcus extended his hand.

"Mr. Webb. A pleasure." The handshake was firm, brief, professional. "Thank you for accommodating us on short notice. Dr. Hadron sends his regards — he's deeply immersed in his current research project and couldn't break away. I'm here to help him prepare for the upcoming initiative."

"Of course, of course. We're happy to assist the university." Marcus gestured toward the corridor. "Shall I show you the unit?"

"Please."

They walked through the facility's main corridor, past storage rooms and deactivated clean rooms, their footsteps echoing off polished concrete. Marcus launched into his standard tour patter, pointing out the original installation date, the power conduit upgrades from 2538, the reinforced containment chamber that had been added after the Phobos Incident.

Dr. Freeman listened attentively, asking precise questions — not the vague queries of a bureaucrat, but the specific, probing inquiries of someone who understood particle physics at a fundamental level. What was the maximum energy output per cycle? Had the magnetic containment coils been recalibrated since the upgrade? What was the thermal dissipation rate during sustained operation?

"The coils were recalibrated eighteen months ago," Marcus said, pleased by the question. "Full superconductor refresh. Most buyers don't even ask about that."

"Dr. Hadron is very particular about magnetic alignment," Dr. Freeman said. "He won't run a cycle unless the field variance is under point-zero-three percent."

Marcus raised an eyebrow. That was an unusually tight tolerance, even for serious research. But he nodded appreciatively. "She can do that. With the 2538 upgrades, we consistently hit point-zero-two."

"Excellent."

Marcus found himself warming to the man. It was rare to meet someone who appreciated the Kepler-7 for what she was — not just a piece of equipment to be tolerated, but a precision instrument worthy of respect.

They reached the main chamber. The accelerator ring dominated the space — a massive circular structure, thirty meters in diameter, encased in layers of superconducting magnets and radiation shielding. Diagnostic panels lined the walls, their displays dormant but ready.

Marcus watched Dr. Freeman take it in. Most visitors stopped at the door, overwhelmed by the scale of the thing. Freeman walked straight to the primary injection port, knelt, and examined the coupling mechanism with the ease of someone who'd seen accelerators before.

"She's not the latest model," Marcus said, running his hand along the outer casing with unconscious affection, "but she'll do exactly what your specifications require. I reviewed the technical parameters Dr. Hadron's office sent over. Full spectrum particle collision capability, adjustable energy levels, and yes — she can generate micro black holes."

Dr. Freeman's eyes sharpened, though his expression remained pleasant. "The black hole generation — what's the duration?"

"Approximately seven milliseconds per event. Brief, but sufficient for most exotic particle research. The containment field handles the rest." Marcus paused, a note of pride creeping into his voice. "The original installation was right next to the main reactor at the Helios station — literally the next block over. She draws an enormous amount of power during operation. That's actually why this facility was built here, adjacent to the power plant. You'll need to ensure your installation site has comparable energy infrastructure."

"Naturally." Dr. Freeman circled the accelerator slowly, his gaze traveling along the ring with the careful attention of a buyer inspecting a very expensive purchase. Which, Marcus supposed, he was. "And the precision controls? We'll need exact calibration for temporal measurements."

"State of the art for her generation. The software was updated in 2541. We can include the full diagnostic suite and calibration tools in the package."

Dr. Freeman nodded, apparently satisfied. He opened his briefcase and produced a tablet, scrolling through what appeared to be a technical checklist. Each item received a precise check mark. Marcus noticed the man's hands were steady — no hesitation, no uncertainty. Whatever this project was, Freeman had done his homework.

"Everything matches our requirements. Dr. Hadron will be pleased."

"I'm glad to hear it. She deserves a good home." Marcus meant it, too. Eleven years of maintaining a machine that nobody used anymore. It was time.

They moved to Marcus's office to handle the paperwork. The contract had been drafted weeks ago — standard transfer of government-tracked research equipment, with the usual regulatory disclosures. Marcus pulled up the logistics module on his terminal.

"Now, about transportation," he said. "We have a long-standing relationship with Ares Precision Logistics. They've handled every accelerator move we've facilitated — specialized containment cradles, vibration dampening, the works. She's a delicate machine, Dr. Freeman. I strongly recommend using our people."

"That won't be necessary." Dr. Freeman's tone was unchanged — the same pleasant, professional register — but the words landed like a door closing. "We have our own transportation arrangements."

Marcus hesitated. "Are you certain? Ares has moved Kepler units before. They know the tolerances. One miscalculated vibration during transit and the magnetic alignment could shift. You'd be looking at a full recalibration before you could run a single cycle."

"Our logistics team is quite experienced. I appreciate the concern, truly, but we have specific protocols for our research facilities." He paused, as if considering whether to share more. "Security clearances, chain-of-custody documentation — the university has become rather cautious about equipment transfers since the incident at Pavonis Mons last year."

Marcus remembered the Pavonis Mons story — a centrifuge stolen during an interlab transfer. It had made the news for a week. The reference was reassuring. Specific. Grounded.

"I understand completely," Marcus said, though a faint unease had settled somewhere behind his ribs. He'd transported dozens of units without incident. But the customer had the right to choose their own logistics. He moved on.

"I'll need the installation address for the government registry. Standard protocol for all particle accelerators — you know how it is. They like to keep track of anything that can generate exotic particles."

Dr. Freeman's pen paused over the contract. It was a small thing — barely a fraction of a second — but Marcus noticed.

"The administrative details are being finalized by the university's facilities department," Dr. Freeman said smoothly. "They'll submit the address directly to the registry within the standard thirty-day window. Dr. Hadron's team is still preparing the installation site."

"Right." Marcus pulled up the warranty section. "We do offer a one-year parts and labor guarantee. If anything goes wrong with the unit, we have a contractual obligation to send an engineer to your site for repairs. We'd need to know where to send them."

"Of course. The address will be provided as soon as the site is confirmed. These things take time with university bureaucracy." A slight, self-deprecating smile. "I'm sure you understand."

Marcus understood. He understood that he'd asked for an address twice and received the same polished deflection both times. He understood that the man sitting across from him had answers for everything — good answers, reasonable answers — but not a single one that actually resolved the question.

He looked at the contract. The university's seal was legitimate. The purchase order number checked out. The credits had already been transferred into escrow. Everything was in order. Everything except the small, persistent itch at the back of his mind that said something here didn't quite fit.

He thought about pushing harder. He thought about saying, I've been doing this for eleven years, and the ones who won't give me an address are never the ones I want to sell to.

But the purchase order was real. The credentials were real. The credits were real. And Marcus was a facility manager on a facility that Helios Corp had been threatening to decommission for the past eighteen months — a facility whose continued existence depended on proving it could still generate revenue. He had a contract to fulfill and a quarterly target that this single sale would comfortably exceed.

Marcus signed the authorization. "She's yours, Dr. Freeman. Or rather, Dr. Hadron's."

"On his behalf, thank you." Dr. Freeman signed with a flourish, stood, and shook Marcus's hand again. "The transport team will contact you within forty-eight hours to arrange collection. It's been a genuine pleasure, Mr. Webb. The Kepler-7 is a remarkable machine."

"She is that." Marcus walked him to the entrance, making small talk about power requirements and cooling systems. At the airlock, Dr. Freeman thanked him again, stepped through, and was gone.

Marcus returned to his office. He sat down, looked at the signed contract, and pulled up the government registry submission form. Installation address: pending.

He told himself it was fine. Universities were slow. Bureaucracy was bureaucracy. Dr. Hadron's reputation was sterling.

But he opened a new message anyway, addressed to the Office of Technology Regulation, and began typing a routine inquiry about the purchase order number. Just to be thorough.

Outside the facility, the man who called himself Dr. Freeman walked past the Helios Power Station without a glance. He reached a nondescript transport pod at the far end of the lot — no university insignia, no institutional markings. He placed his briefcase on the seat, removed the thin prosthetic film that had subtly altered his jawline and cheekbones, and dropped it into a disposal unit.

He flexed his jaw, working out the stiffness from two hours of wearing the prosthetic. His real face was younger, sharper, with none of the academic softness he'd projected inside. He checked the rearview — no one following — and allowed himself a thin smile.

Then he placed a call. No video. Voice only.

"The accelerator is secured. Collection in forty-eight hours."

A pause on the other end. Then: "Any complications?"

"None. The manager filed the standard paperwork. No address on record. The registry inquiry will dead-end at the university's administrative queue — our contact there will ensure it stays buried."

"Good. And the power requirements?"

"Significant. But that won't be our problem for much longer."

The call ended. Dr. Freeman — or whatever his real name was — started the pod and drove north, toward the cargo terminals, where a ship was being quietly prepared for a very long journey to the outer solar system.

Behind him, in the quiet of the Prometheus Facility, the Kepler-7 sat in its chamber. Thirty meters of superconducting magnets and containment fields, capable of tearing holes in the very fabric of spacetime — seven milliseconds at a time.

Marcus Webb sat at his desk, staring at the pending address field on his registry form. His inquiry to the Office of Technology Regulation would take weeks to process. By then, the accelerator would be long gone.

He told himself, again, that it was fine.

The itch at the back of his mind told him otherwise.
