Chapter 1: Echoes of Extinction

The dinosaur skull was larger than Sarah Parker remembered.

She stood in the Hall of Deep Time, Earth's Natural History Museum, staring up at the reconstructed Tyrannosaurus rex that dominated the central atrium. It was the same specimen she'd seen fifteen years ago on her first visit — the same fossilized grin, the same rows of serrated teeth — but something about the scale of it seemed different now. Bigger. More final. The empty eye sockets gazed past her with the blank authority of something that had owned the world for a hundred and sixty million years and then, in a geological instant, had not.

Behind her, twenty-three Martian-born children shuffled and whispered, their excitement barely contained. For most of them, this was their first time on Earth. The field trip had been approved six months ago — a rare educational excursion from Olympus Academy, funded by the Colonial Education Initiative. Sarah had written the grant application herself, arguing that no holographic simulation could replace standing in the physical presence of Earth's deep history. The committee had agreed, though she suspected the real reason was that Earth needed the tourism revenue.

"All right, everyone, gather round," Sarah said, and her voice carried across the hall, amplified softly by the students' neural implants. She didn't need to shout. The implants were standard issue for Martian-born children — communication aids that compensated for the physiological differences that three centuries of low-gravity adaptation had produced.

The children who gathered before her did not look entirely like the humans of Old Earth. Their eyes were wider, multi-faceted, with nictitating membranes that slid sideways when they blinked — an adaptation to Mars's thinner atmosphere and harsher radiation. Their limbs were longer, their frames lighter. A few of the older students had the beginnings of the bioluminescent skin patterns that would fully develop in puberty — subcutaneous chromatophores that served as a secondary communication channel, expressing emotion in slow pulses of color whether the bearer wanted them to or not.

Sarah's own patterns flickered faintly at the base of her neck. She'd learned to control them years ago, but grief had a way of slipping through.

She looked at the skull again. Ben had loved this museum. He'd been seven on their last visit — tugging at her hand, running ahead, pressing his face against every display case while her husband Mark laughed and tried to keep up. The three of them had spent an entire afternoon in this hall. Mark had known the name of every species, every geological period, every mass extinction. He'd told Ben that the dinosaurs hadn't really died — they'd just become birds. Ben had spent the rest of the day flapping his arms.

That was eight years ago. Before the accident. Before Sarah had moved back to Mars with nothing but a teaching certificate and the kind of hollow resilience that comes from having no other choice.

She cleared her throat.

"This," she said, gesturing to the skull above them, "is a Tyrannosaurus rex. It lived approximately sixty-eight million years ago, during the late Cretaceous period, on the planet you're standing on right now." She let that sink in. Some of the children looked down at their feet, as if expecting the floor to feel different.

"The dinosaurs ruled Earth for over a hundred and sixty million years. They were the most successful large animals this planet has ever produced. And then, sixty-six million years ago, they were gone." She activated the holographic display with a gesture. The air above the children erupted with light — a simulation of the Chicxulub impact, rendered in real-time physics. The asteroid — ten kilometers wide, moving at twenty kilometers per second — struck the Yucatan Peninsula in a flash of white. Shockwaves rippled outward. Dust and debris fountained into the upper atmosphere. The children gasped, their neural implants feeding them supplementary data: impact energy, ejecta velocity, thermal pulse radius.

"An asteroid," Sarah continued, "knocked off course by a collision in the ancient Kuiper Belt. It traveled for millions of years through empty space before its path intersected with Earth's. A one-in-a-million occurrence. Just the right place, at just the right time."

The simulation cycled through the aftermath — the fires, the nuclear winter, the slow die-off. The children watched in silence. One girl in the front row had her nictitating membranes fully extended, a reflex that Sarah recognized as distress.

"Ms. Parker?" A voice from the back. Small, precise, curious.

Sarah smiled. She didn't need to look to know who it was. "Yes, Timmy?"

Little Timmy — Timothy Osei, age nine, gifted with an enhanced cognitive lobe that made him the most relentless question-asker in any classroom he'd ever entered — pushed to the front of the group. His tendrils, thin sensory appendages that grew from the temples of some third-generation Martian children, waved gently as he thought.

"Why did the asteroid fly toward Earth specifically?"

"Well, Timmy, as I said — the Kuiper Belt contains thousands of objects constantly interacting with each other. Gravitational perturbations, collisions, orbital resonances. This particular asteroid was deflected onto a trajectory that happened to intersect with Earth's orbit. It wasn't aimed. It wasn't intentional. It was just physics."

Timmy considered this. His tendrils curled, then straightened. "But Ms. Parker — what if the asteroid had missed?"

The question stopped her. Not because it was difficult — it wasn't — but because of the way Timmy asked it. As if the answer mattered enormously, and as if he already suspected what it was.

"Well, I..." Sarah's bioluminescent patterns pulsed faintly — surprise, leaking through despite her control. "I suppose if the asteroid had missed, the dinosaurs might have survived. The mass extinction wouldn't have occurred, or at least not in the same way. The ecological niches that mammals eventually filled would have remained occupied. Evolution would have taken a very different path." She paused. "I'm not certain what that path would have looked like. It's not really my area."

"I can help with that."

The voice came from behind her — deep, resonant, and entirely uninvited. Sarah turned sharply.

A tall man stood a few meters away, hands in the pockets of a rumpled tweed jacket. He had the look of someone who had been standing there for a while, listening, and had only now decided to speak. His eyes held an odd light — not quite amusement, not quite sadness. Something older than both.

"If that asteroid had missed," the stranger said, his gaze moving from Sarah to the T. rex skull and back, "those magnificent beasts would still be roaming this planet. Earth would be a lush, verdant paradise — quite unlike the environmental challenges we face today." He smiled, and the smile didn't reach his eyes. "No ice ages driven by mammalian deforestation. No industrial revolution. No carbon wars. No exodus to Mars." He glanced at the children. "No need for multi-faceted eyes or neural implants."

The children stared at him, wide-eyed. A few exchanged glances. Timmy's tendrils had gone very still.

"It's just one of a million possibilities, of course," the stranger added, his tone shifting to something lighter, almost dismissive. He laughed — a dry, quiet sound. But the lightness didn't match the weight of what he'd just said, and Sarah felt the dissonance like a wrong note in a familiar song.

"Excuse me," she said, keeping her voice steady, her teacher-authority firmly in place. "Are you a docent here? I wasn't told we'd have a guest speaker."

"Just a visitor," the man said. "Like you." He nodded to the children. "Enjoy the rest of your tour. This is a remarkable place. What's left of it."

He turned and walked toward the far end of the hall, his footsteps fading into the ambient hum of the museum's climate systems.

Sarah watched him go. Something about the encounter nagged at her — not what he'd said, exactly, but the certainty with which he'd said it. As if he wasn't speculating about a world without the asteroid. As if he was remembering one.

She shook it off. "All right, everyone. Let's keep moving. We have the Cenozoic wing next, and I promised Ms. Rodriguez we'd be back at the shuttle bay by fourteen hundred hours."

The children fell into line, chattering among themselves. Timmy lingered at the back, staring at the holographic asteroid still frozen mid-impact above the display case. He reached out a hand, fingers spread, as if trying to catch it — or push it off course.

Sarah smiled at the sight. It reminded her of Ben.

"Come on, Timmy. Lots more to see."

He lowered his hand and followed.

As they moved into the next gallery, Sarah glanced back once toward the spot where the stranger had been standing. The hall was empty. She tried to recall his face — his features, his age, anything specific — and found, with a flicker of unease, that she couldn't. She remembered the tweed jacket. She remembered the voice. But the face was already gone, as if it had never quite been there.

She turned back to her students and didn't think about it again.
