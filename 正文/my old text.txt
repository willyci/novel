Pro log


An unassuming asteroid collides with its celestial neighbor in the silent void of space. Witnessless and nameless, it loses a few fragments upon impact. A small piece breaks off, drifting toward the inner solar system. 
Its trajectory carries it through the cosmic expanse, a mote adrift in the vastness of space. 
Perhaps it will continue its solitary journey for eons, cold and unnoticed. Or maybe it will be drawn into a planet's gravitational embrace, becoming a fleeting streak of light in an alien sky. 
The fragment tumbles onward, indifferent to its fate. In the grand cosmic ballet, it plays but a tiny, forgettable role—one of countless bits of matter scattered across the indifferent universe.

A drama unfolding in the vast indifference of the universe.



Chapter 1: Echoes of Extinction
Sarah Parker stood motionless before the colossal dinosaur skull, its empty eye sockets seeming to bore into her very soul. The sight transported her back to happier times—her son Ben's wide-eyed wonder, her husband's enthusiastic explanations. Both of them had shared a passion for these prehistoric beasts. Now, that shared joy was nothing more than a bittersweet memory.
Shaking off the nostalgia, Sarah turned to face her class. The field trip to Earth's Natural History Museum was a rare treat for these Martian-born children. Their altered physiologies—multi-faceted eyes and nictitating membranes—were a stark reminder of how far humanity had come. Or perhaps, how far they'd been forced to go.
"And so, young ones," Sarah's voice carried through the cavernous hall, amplified by the students' neural implants, "this is how the dinosaurs of Old Earth met their end. An asteroid, knocked off course from the ancient Kuiper Belt, collided with the planet, resulting in a catastrophic extinction event."
The holographic display flickered to life, showing Earth's violent collision with the massive space rock. The children gasped collectively, their augmented minds struggling to comprehend the scale of such destruction.
"Why did the asteroid fly toward Earth?" piped up a voice from the back.
Sarah smiled, recognizing the insatiable curiosity of Little Timmy, a child gifted with an extra brain lobe for enhanced problem-solving. "Well, Timmy, there are thousands of asteroids in the Kuiper Belt, constantly colliding with each other. This particular event was a one-in-a-million occurrence—just the right place, at just the right time."
Timmy's tendrils waved in thought before he asked, "But Ms. Parker, what if the asteroid had missed Earth?"
The question caught Sarah off guard. Her bioluminescent patterns flickered, betraying her surprise. "Well, I... I'm not entirely certain, Timmy. I've never really considered it. I suppose a lot of things could have changed?"

Before Sarah could elaborate, a deep, resonant voice cut through the air. "If that asteroid had missed, those magnificent beasts would still be roaming our planet. Earth would be a lush, verdant paradise—quite unlike the environmental challenges we face today."
Sarah whirled around, startled by the interruption. A tall man in a rumpled tweed jacket stood nearby, his eyes twinkling with an odd light.
The stranger's words hung in the air as the children exchanged excited glances, their young minds racing with visions of an alternate Earth.
A sudden quietness fell over the kids, broken only by the stranger's dry laugh. "It's just one of a million possibilities!" he added, his voice tinged with an emotion Sarah couldn't quite place.
Shaking off her momentary confusion, Sarah clapped her hands to regain her students' attention. "All right, kids, let's keep moving. We have a lot more to see before we leave!" She began ushering the children towards the next exhibition, her teacher instincts kicking in despite the odd interruption.
As the class moved on, Sarah noticed Timmy lagging behind, his hand outstretched as if trying to catch the holographic asteroid. The sight brought a small smile to her face, reminding her of Ben's endless fascination with space.
"Mia, try to catch up with the class!" Sarah called out to a girl who had paused to examine a nearby display. As she herded the stragglers forward, Sarah didn't realize that she had already forgotten what the stranger looked like.

Chapter 2: Missing Nanomachines

Bob glanced at his watch, anxiety creeping into his voice. "Come on, Steve, it's all here. We've been doing this every two weeks for three years. I've never missed anything!" He ran a hand through his unkempt hair, thinking about his impending date. "You're making me late for my date with Mary!"

Steve, ever the stickler for protocol, mumbled as he meticulously checked each item. "I don't want to lose my job because you're feeling lovey-dovey! I have to check and log everything."

Suddenly, Steve's methodical process came to an abrupt halt. "Oh shit," he muttered, his face paling.

Bob's heart sank. "What is it? I didn't break anything, not even hit a bump on the road. Don't pin this on me."

Steve held up a glass tube, a hairline fracture running its length. "The glass tube is broken. All the others are fine. It must have been damaged during packaging."

Bob peered at the tube, confusion etched on his face. "What's in there? They all look empty to me."

Sighing, Steve consulted his inventory list. "Let's see... ah, here it is. They're 'self-replicating asteroid mining machines.'"

"What are you talking about? Those glass tubes look empty!" Bob exclaimed, his patience wearing thin.

"They're nanomachines, you can't see them," Steve explained, a hint of excitement creeping into his voice. "They're the latest tech. Super cheap to deploy to any asteroid's surface compared to the old way. Instead of sending bulky machines via spaceship, they just fire these from a cannon, like a bullet. Once it smashes on the asteroid surface, thousands of nanomachines emerge. They search and gather resources, replicate themselves, build bigger machines, conduct surveys, refine materials, and ship ingots back to Mars. All automated. It's going to replace all the space mining jobs soon."

Bob whistled, impressed despite his urgency to leave. "As long as one of them survives the landing, it can start working?"

Steve nodded. "Exactly. It takes forever with just one, so they shoot quite a few tubes to speed things up."

"Can we scoop them back into the tube?" Bob asked, hopeful for a quick solution.

"Nah, don't worry. They're harmless," Steve reassured him. "Designed to function only in specific environments and require a complex activation process. Without activation, they're just like dust."

Relief washed over Bob's face. "Alright, alright, sounds really cool. But I'm not taking the fall for this. There's no damage on the case, nothing happened on the trip."

"Yeah, relax," Steve said, already updating his log. "I'll just record it as damaged during packaging. They can ask for more in the next batch."

"Thanks, man. I owe you one," Bob said, already backing towards the door. "Let me buy you a beer next time."

"Sure thing," Steve replied, waving him off.

"Now," Steve said, his voice tinged with anticipation, "give me what you promised."
Without a word, the man handed over a small box with holes on the top. Bob opened it gingerly, and a hamster poked its head out, its tiny eyes wide with fear.

Joe watching from the sidelines, couldn't help but interject. "Why do you want that thing? I heard they chew on cables and make a mess everywhere. They can lock you up for 100 sols for it."
Steve's face softened as he gazed at the creature. "I've always wanted one since I was a kid. Look at its tiny ears!"

Joe shrugged, already turning to leave. "Whatever. I'm going to burn all the DNA traces on my hands and in my apartment. you should do the same!"

Joe disappeared into the Martian night, and Steve cradled his new pet, oblivious to the potential consequences of their transaction.

Book Title: "0.01 Degree": A Sci-Fi Story of Time Travel, Technology, and Moral Dilemmas

Any feedback is welcome, Thanks ahead!

________________________________

Prologue: A Catastrophic Future

In the year 2543, humanity's reckless exploitation of Earth's resources has reached catastrophic levels, leading to an environmental collapse. As a result, Mars has become the new home for scientific research, hosting advanced projects in nanotechnology, black holes, and energy storage. Meanwhile, the Kuiper Belt, the distant frontier of our solar system, is a bustling hub for mining companies extracting precious resources from asteroids.

The Heist: Stealing "Sun 2.0"

In a bold and desperate move, a group of zealous animal lovers steals a powerful experimental energy source, "Sun 2.0," from a Mars laboratory. They also abscond with an old particle accelerator and a batch of self-replicating AI nanomachines. These stolen technologies are key to their radical plan: to save the dinosaurs by eradicating the human race before it can cause any harm.

The Protagonists: Radical Animal Lovers

These protagonists, driven by their love for animals and hatred for humanity's destructive behavior, establish a secret laboratory on an asteroid in the Kuiper Belt. Their plan is ambitious: they aim to create a micro black hole to send a nanomachine back in time, altering the course of history to prevent the rise of humans.

The Evil Plan: Manipulating Time and Space

Creating a micro black hole requires an enormous energy source that can release all its energy in an instant. By using "Sun 2.0," they generate the necessary conditions to open a man-made micro black hole. The nanomachine passes through it, emerging 67 million years in the past. Its mission is to find the asteroid destined to hit Earth and kill the dinosaurs, and to alter its trajectory by 0.01 degrees to prevent this extinction event.

The nanomachine, designed for mining operations, uses its capabilities to gather materials and energy, self-replicating over centuries to build a functional satellite. This satellite, equipped with scanning and ion propulsion systems, represents the pinnacle of futuristic nanotechnology.

The Hero: Peter, the Programmer

Peter, a planetary navigation programmer, is hired under the guise of working for an asteroid mining company. His task is to write a program to control a satellite capable of altering an asteroid's trajectory. However, Peter grows suspicious when he realizes the star positions used for calculations date back 67 million years. Upon discovering the true nature of the project, he secretly inserts a bug into the AI's programming. This bug ensures that if the time is set in the past, the trajectory adjustment will be reversed, altering it by -0.01 degrees instead.

The Outcome: Unintended Consequences

The nanomachine begins its mission, mining minerals and extracting hydrogen to power itself. Over centuries, it self-replicates and transforms into a fully functional satellite. After nearly a million years, it detects the asteroid on a collision course with Earth. With insufficient power to destroy it, the satellite uses its ion propulsion system to alter the asteroid's trajectory by 0.01 degrees using its gravitational pull.

However, due to Peter's sabotage, the AI directs the satellite to the opposite side of the asteroid, changing its trajectory by -0.01 degrees. The satellite, unable to check its course while in the asteroid's shadow, realizes its error only after passing Jupiter. By then, it is too late to correct its path, and the asteroid collides with Earth, leading to the mass extinction of the dinosaurs and paving the way for the age of mammals and, eventually, humans.

Current Time: A Failed Plan and New Schemes

As soon as the nanomachine passes through the black hole, the group realizes their failure. Their radical plan has backfired, ensuring the very outcome they sought to prevent. Despite their defeat, they begin devising a new, even more sinister plan to achieve their goals.

Conclusion: The Moral and Philosophical Implications

"0.01 Degree" is a sci-fi story that combines elements of time travel, advanced technology, and moral philosophy. It explores the ethical complexities of altering history and the unforeseen consequences that can arise from such interventions. Through its engaging narrative and rich thematic content, the story invites audiences to reflect on the nature of existence, the value of different forms of life, and the responsibilities that come with technological power.

Ultimately, "0.01 Degree" serves as a cautionary tale about the limits of human control over nature and the potential for unintended consequences when meddling with the past. It underscores the resilience of life and the inevitability of evolutionary processes, reminding us of the profound impact of our actions on the future.
