## Plot Summary
Sol 44. Peter has settled into a four-morning routine in the Nav Lab where his workstation runs perturbation simulations against epoch references he has not authorized; he has been closing a yellow reference-frame anachronism flag twice a day and lying to himself that the architecture will eventually make sense. Samir intercepts him with second coffee in hand: "the project lead would like to meet you" — phrasing that reorganizes Peter's mental map, since he had assumed Samir was the project lead. They walk a new corridor past a stenciled MANUFACTURING door and an open bay where three programming stations face a clear-walled containment full of gray-flour medium rippling slowly; a man in fingerless gloves types self-replicating-routine code and presses two fingertips to a containment port the way a parent touches a sleeping child's forehead. Dr. Elena Vasquez (Accelerator Integration) emerges from a side passage, briefs Peter on integration parameters in a clipped technical register, and gives a tell — "Higher than you'll need" — when he asks the bus rating. A short, tired-eyed lab assistant carrying two paper cups passes by calling "Morning, Professor" toward the open office door.

Peter enters Arthur Kessler's office: not a startup CEO's curated theater but three walls of well-read paper books and a fourth wall of terrestrial expedition photos (coral reef, low-tide shoreline, jungle clearing, mossy survey camp). On the bare desk: a working terminal showing the trajectory simulation, and a single fragment of pale branched coral on a small glass stand, no label. Kessler is sixty-ish, iron-gray hair, dark unhurried eyes, the physical economy of someone who lived in the field. He greets Peter warmly, insists on "Arthur," and immediately discusses Peter's perturbation cluster commit with informed pleasure — "It makes the failure mode legible" — and tells him three other people had looked at that architecture and none touched the anchors. Warmth comes up under Peter's ribs before he can intercept it. Kessler murmurs, almost to himself, "I used to teach. Students like you would have made my office hours bearable." They talk thirty minutes; Kessler asks about Helix, the de-orbit work, the six months after, and Peter's grandfather's name without pity-curiosity. He uses phrases like *deep time*, *epoch-true reference*, and *operations on geological scales* in the way other people say *quarterly*. As Peter rises to leave, Kessler hesitates — small enough Peter will later not be sure he saw it — and says: anything in the code that doesn't make sense, bring directly to me. Not Samir. The code is mine, not his.

Peter walks back through the now-familiar corridors, past the gray-flour bay and the still-typing programmer, and sits at his workstation a long time without opening anything. Then he reopens the project specs, rereads the reference-epoch section, confirms the nav code is required to maintain stellar drift correction across reference frames spanning *millions of years* — not a typo, not a units error, no commercial mining trajectory has ever required this. He thinks about Kessler's hands when he said *operations on geological scales*, the coral fragment, the gloved man's parental gesture. He makes himself look directly at the part he had been avoiding: he likes Arthur Kessler, and he wants the project to make sense, and these two things are not the same thing. He takes the paper notepad from the drawer (an affectation in 2543, here for fire drills) and writes three lines in his small square print: *Why does the nav require stellar positions millions of years deep? / Whose mining trajectory has ever needed that? / Ask Arthur. Tomorrow morning. First thing.* He turns the page facedown and sets the notepad to the left of the keyboard. The yellow warning flag flickers once, almost shyly, and resolves.
